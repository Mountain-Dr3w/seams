I've been a designer for nearly a decade now. Product design, service design, roles where you spend weeks mapping out how something should behave and then write it up in a way that helps someone else go build it. I loved it. I loved the systems thinking, and I loved being the person in the room who could talk to users and come back with the thing nobody had named yet. That part of the job still matters to me.

But when I did that for long enough, I started to really notice how many ideas would just sit there. You put together a journey map, a service blueprint, or a prototype and it goes into a backlog. Sometimes it gets built. Sometimes it gets half-built, or built later in a way that doesn't match what you intended, or it just never moves because the engineering team had other priorities. Your work ends up living in Figma files and Miro boards and decks that people nod at in meetings and then forget about. Every designer has ideas that never make it to implementation. I don't think I understood how much that was wearing on me until I started being able to do something about it.

## picking up the tools

In short, AI coding tools got good. Good enough, even, that someone with my background, who understands how software should work but had rarely written production code, could start building functional applications. Real ones, with logins and databases and deployment pipelines. Things people can actually use.

I should be specific about what that process looked like, because I think there's a temptation to make it sound like a montage but it wasn't. I described features in plain language, the AI generated code. A lot of that code was broken or weird or structured in ways I didn't understand (ask me about the onboarding portal I vibe coded. Better yet, ask the engineers on my team that had to fix it), but I iterated on it, asked questions about it, and slowly started recognizing patterns and developing taste for what good code looked like even when I couldn't write it from scratch. I spent months feeling slightly lost inside my own project. It was also the most fun I've had at a computer in years, and I keep thinking about why those two things happened at the same time.

I think it's because building changed how I viewed design. When you're the one making the thing, it breaks your assumptions in ways you never encounter when you're not involved in implementation. You realize your clean onboarding flow needs to account for what happens when an API call takes four seconds instead of one, and that the loading state you never designed for is actually where users spend most of their time. That kind of feedback has always existed but I was a level removed from it. There's a difference between reading about an issue and watching your own build fall over because of it.

There's a concept in woodworking and ceramics about having a conversation with your material. A woodworker doesn't just execute a plan because the grain of the wood has opinions and the good work comes from responding to them. I think design as a field has been operating at a distance from its material for a while now. We got very sophisticated at that distance, but something was missing and building is what gave it back to me.

## where the field is going

I have opinions about where the design field is headed. They're convenient opinions for someone in my position, but I think they're right.

I first entered the design field as part of the Air Force's Kessel Run, and since then the design career path has been moving toward more abstraction. You start out as someone who makes things and you "grow" into someone who makes strategy decks about things. More frameworks, more workshops, more operating models. As you progress through your career you move upstream, away from making and toward orchestrating how things get made. That made sense when building was expensive and required years of specialized training. The most leverage a designer could get was in shaping what the team built so engineering effort didn't go to waste.

But building isn't expensive like that anymore and when the cost of making something drops as dramatically as it has in the last two years, being the person who only shapes and never ships gets harder to justify. I don't think that role goes away entirely. Complex systems still need people who can hold the whole picture in their head and think clearly about it, but the premium is shifting toward people who can hold the whole picture and then go build a working version of it. The person who can go from noticing a problem to shipping a fix without a handoff is extremely useful, and that usefulness is only going to increase.

I was the anxious version of this for a long time, positioned as the person who thinks about a thing and then waits for someone else to make it. That identity worked for me, and letting go of it was hard. The designers I talk to who are energized right now are the ones who picked up Cursor or Claude six months ago and started building alongside their design work. Shipping internal tools, prototyping in real code instead of Figma, standing up small apps that solve specific problems their teams have. I don't think that's a coincidence.

I **WANT** the field to go in this direction. I think it's probably going there regardless of what anyone wants, but I'm not a neutral observer and I'm not going to pretend to be. The version of design that excites me is one where designers build. I want to take something from idea to live product myself. The deeper I get into building, the more respect I have for engineering as a discipline. But the best design work I've ever done has been the stuff I also helped build.

Real quick notes on titles: I don't necessarily think everyone will be called "builders" in the future. There's probably a breakout of titles that make more sense, but I also don't think it matters. I've been an ISSO, a product designer, a service designer, a PM. The title changed every time but the work didn't: find a problem, figure out why it's broken for the people living with it, and make it better. I don't think the next title matters. The work does.

## velveteen

The name Velveteen comes from The Velveteen Rabbit, a children's book about a toy that becomes real through being genuinely loved and used. (I have a one year old daughter who loves books, so this name came naturally.) I named it that because the apps people are building with AI tools right now remind me of that story. They mean something. Someone cared enough about a problem to sit down and describe a solution and iterate on it until it worked on their laptop. Where it falls apart is getting from "works on my machine" to "works in the world." That gap is almost entirely an infrastructure and security problem.

I kept hitting that wall myself. I'd build something I was proud of and then spend days fighting with Docker, environment variables, deployment pipelines, all stuff that has nothing to do with whether the app is "good" or solves a real problem. Going through this myself, I started noticing that the people hitting this wall are often the ones building the most interesting things, because they're building from domain expertise rather than engineering background. Teachers making classroom tools. Nonprofit workers automating processes that eat up their week. People who understand their problem space better than any off-the-shelf tool ever will, and just need the last mile to not be a cliff.

Now on to Velveteen. Velveteen is what I'm building to handle that last mile. You connect your GitHub, pick a repo, and it figures out your framework, generates the infrastructure files you're missing, runs a security pipeline, and explains anything it finds in language you can actually do something with. When everything passes, it deploys to a live URL. I wrote more about the specifics on the [about page](https://velveteen.sh/about), and the full technical breakdown is [under the hood](https://velveteen.sh/about/under-the-hood).

I keep thinking about what happens when that barrier comes down for a lot of people at once. Thousands of small, specific apps getting into the hands of the people they were built for. That's what I think about when I'm working on this before my day job starts.

## what comes next

This blog is going to be where I document the whole process. Technical decisions, design decisions, the places where those end up being the same decision. Things I got wrong and had to redo. What it's actually like going from someone who makes recommendations for a living to someone who ships software, with all the humbling parts of that transition included.

If you're a non-technical person who's been thinking about picking up a coding tool, or someone who already started building and isn't sure what comes next, I don't have it figured out, but I'd rather write about it now while it's messy than wait until the story is clean enough to be useless.
