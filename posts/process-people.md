# Process People

*Seams — Blog No. 02 | April 2026*

*On the difference between owning a step and owning a problem, and what happens when the steps start disappearing.*

---

## The room where it happens

I've sat in a lot of meetings where someone's job was clearly to make sure the process happened. Not to make sure the outcome was good, but to make sure the right sequence of reviews, handoffs, sign-offs occurred in the right order. This can be valuable and necessary, especially in large organizations where things fall through cracks and someone needs to hold the thread. I don't want to dismiss that because things fall apart when nobody does it.

But there's another version that I think is very dangerous, where the process itself becomes the point. Where someone's professional identity gets so wrapped up in a specific framework or workflow that protecting that thing starts to matter more than whether the workflow is producing good results. A designer might fight to keep a research phase in a project timeline not because the research was going to change the outcome, but because removing it felt like an attack on the value of research as a discipline. A PM might insist on a prioritization framework that everyone knew was theater, because running the framework was how they "performed" their role. The framework wasn't necessarily wrong, exactly. It just wasn't changing any decisions, and everyone could feel it.

I think anyone in knowledge work has been in rooms like this. Transitioning to a world where a large part of what we build is facilitated by "the magic genie" (term stolen from Kent Beck), I've been trying to figure out what happens to those rooms when AI compresses the workflow.

## Translation layers

When a designer can go from problem identification to working prototype in an afternoon instead of a quarter, a lot of intermediate steps either shrink or vanish. The journey map that took two weeks can get a reasonable first draft in minutes with good research inputs. The design system audit that justified a three-sprint initiative is automatable. I wrote in my [first post](https://seams.velveteen.sh/becoming-a-builder) about what happened when I started building software myself. What I didn't fully say there is how many of my own design artifacts stopped being necessary once I could just make the thing and test it.

Don't get me wrong: These steps weren't useless. They existed because there was a gap between understanding a problem and being able to build a solution, and someone had to manage that gap. The journey map was a translation layer between user insight and engineering action. The spec document was the only reliable way to transmit design intent across a handoff. Real problems, real artifacts that solved them, and people who built careers around that skill. I was one of them. I got good at service blueprints specifically because they were an artifact that let me influence what got built without needing to build it myself.

The gap is closing. When the distance between problem understanding and a working solution shrinks, those translation layers change. Some are unnecessary, and some need to evolve to communicate with both human and LLM (that tension between human-readable and machine-readable artifacts is probably my next article). Not all of them change overnight, but they've changed enough that it matters.

## The blueprint I didn't need

I noticed this in myself before I noticed it anywhere else. When I started building software directly, I kept reaching for the service blueprint as a first move, even in situations where I could have just built the thing and tested whether it worked. Some of that was good instinct. Thinking before building is smart. But some of it was me protecting the part of my identity that was "the person who makes great service blueprints" instead of asking what would move the problem forward.

I'm still catching myself doing this. This week I planned research activities that, when I reflected honestly on it, weren't going to change the outcome or the team's decision. I had to kill them before we executed. That instinct doesn't go away just because you can name it.

In several cases the answer was to skip the blueprint, build a rough version, put it in front of someone, and learn more in that single conversation than the blueprint would have told me. Letting go of it felt like giving something up, even though what I was giving up was a constraint, not a capability. That's the thing that makes this moment hard for a lot of practitioners. The process steps we built our identities around were useful (and in the right situation still are) and now some of them are becoming optional. Both of those are true at the same time.

## Incentive structures

There's a distinction here between people who follow a process because they care about the outcome it's supposed to produce and people who follow a process because the process is their job. The first group swaps methods the moment a better one appears. The second group experiences that swap as a threat, because the method was the thing they thought was their job.

That's not like a personality flaw or anything. Bad organizations train people into it. You get promoted for running a workshop well rather than for whether the workshop led to a better product, and you learn quickly that the workshop is what matters. Your performance review measures whether you produced a research report rather than whether the research changed a decision, so you optimize for the report. Of course people attach their identity to process. The system told them to.

AI is stress testing those structures in a way nothing else has. When a tool can produce the artifact in a fraction of the time, the artifact stops being a proxy for the thinking behind it. A journey map generated in ten minutes doesn't carry the same implicit signal of rigor that one produced over two weeks does, even if the content is equivalent. Organizations were using the artifact to evaluate expertise, practitioners were using it to demonstrate expertise, and I think that whole arrangement is broken. I think the answer is evaluating people on the quality of the problems they identify and whether their work changes outcomes, regardless of what artifacts they did or didn't produce along the way. That sounds obvious when you say it out loud, but almost nobody's performance review actually works that way. Quick shout out to my company, Rise8, here. They evaluate based on the outcomes and impact of the work. It's one of the reasons I love it there. But it's rare, and most organizations are still measuring activity.

## What problem do you solve?

The designers I know who are navigating this new world well all have a clear answer to that question that doesn't reference a specific deliverable or process step. "I figure out why users abandon a workflow and I fix it." "I make sure the product actually works for the people who have to use it every day." The method stays flexible. Some of them have picked up coding tools like Claude Code and started building directly. Others have found different ways to get closer to the material, or closer to the actual thing their users interact with, rather than staying upstream of it. Either way, they can let go of a method without feeling like they've lost their footing.

Going from "I have a defined design process" to "I solve service delivery problems, sometimes with blueprints and sometimes by building software and sometimes by doing something I haven't figured out yet" is disorienting. This isn't exclusive to process changes with AI, it's just part of becoming a better designer. I think that disorientation is a useful signal, but it's uncomfortable while you're in it. It's a good uncomfortable. If anyone is reading this and feeling that discomfort, embrace it. That discomfort is what accompanies growth. It's definitely not a novel or original idea, but if you aren't comfortable with this feeling of discomfort, then you're in the wrong field.

I talked in [Becoming a Builder](https://seams.velveteen.sh/becoming-a-builder) about having a conversation with your material, an idea from woodworking and ceramics. Building on that idea, I really think the process-identity problem is a distance problem. When you're far from the material, process is how you maintain influence over what gets built. When you're close enough to work with it directly, you need less of it. You can feel whether the thing is working or not. That doesn't mean every designer needs to become an engineer. It means finding ways to shorten the loop between your ideas and something real, whatever that looks like for you.
