I've been a designer for a long time. I know how to make things look right, feel right, communicate the right things. I know how to hand off a Figma file and explain the intent behind every decision.

What I didn't know how to do was make things real.

Not in the way that matters — not the "put it on the internet, have real people use it" kind of real. That part always required someone else. A developer, a team, a sprint, a ticket. The idea would leave my hands and come back weeks later, slightly different from what I'd imagined, shaped by constraints I didn't fully understand.

## the gap I kept running into

There's a specific frustration that designers who work closely with developers know well. You understand the problem better than anyone. You've done the research, you've talked to the users, you've thought carefully about what the interface needs to do. But you can't fully own the outcome because the last mile — the part where the thing becomes real and reaches people — requires skills you don't have.

I got tired of that gap. So I started closing it.

## what actually changed

The first thing that changed was my relationship to constraints. When you design without building, infrastructure is invisible. You make decisions without understanding what they cost, and that makes you less useful than you could be.

When you start building, the constraints become real. You learn that some things you thought were design problems are actually architecture problems, and some things you thought were architecture problems have design solutions. That feedback loop — making something, watching it break, understanding why — is irreplaceable.

The second thing that changed was how I think about scope. Designers are trained to think comprehensively. You consider every state, every edge case, every user type. That skill is valuable, but it can be paralyzing when you're trying to ship. Building teaches you to ask: what's the smallest thing that's actually real? What can I put in front of a person today?

## what I'm building

This site is a dispatch — dispatches from that process. Things I'm learning, things I'm building, case studies of what worked and what didn't. I'm not trying to become a developer in the traditional sense. I'm trying to close the gap between what I can imagine and what I can make real.

If you're a designer thinking about the same thing, or a builder who cares about the design side: I think we have a lot to learn from each other.
